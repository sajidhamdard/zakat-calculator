Gold vs Silver Nisab – Which Should You Follow in India?

A clear, practical guide for Indian Muslims — understand both thresholds in ₹ and find out which one applies to you

Every year, when Ramadan approaches, millions of Indian Muslims start thinking about Zakat. One of the most common questions people ask is: "Should I use the gold Nisab or the silver Nisab?"

This question matters because the two Nisab values are very different in rupee terms. One is around ₹55,000. The other is closer to ₹7,00,000. That is a massive gap — and it decides whether you are obligated to pay Zakat or not.

This article will explain both options in simple language, give you real INR examples, and help you understand which one most scholars in India recommend.

What Is Nisab, and Why Does It Matter?

Nisab is the minimum amount of wealth you must own before Zakat becomes obligatory for you. Think of it as a starting line. If your total zakatable wealth is below this line, you do not have to pay Zakat. If it is at or above this line — and you have held that wealth for one full lunar year — Zakat becomes compulsory.

There are two ways the Nisab is set: one based on gold, and one based on silver. Both come from Islamic tradition, and both are accepted by scholars. The difference is simply in the metal they use as a reference point.

What Is the Gold Nisab?

The Weight

The gold Nisab is 87.48 grams of gold (sometimes written as 7.5 tola or 3 oz). This weight has not changed — it is fixed based on Islamic scholarship.

The Value in INR (2026)

Gold prices in India fluctuate regularly. As of early 2026, gold is trading at roughly ₹7,200–₹8,000 per gram (for 24-carat gold).

  • At ₹7,500/g → Gold Nisab = 87.48 × ₹7,500 = approx. ₹6,56,100
  • At ₹8,000/g → Gold Nisab = 87.48 × ₹8,000 = approx. ₹6,99,840

So the gold Nisab in India right now is roughly ₹6.5 lakh to ₹7 lakh. This means only those whose total zakatable wealth (cash + gold + silver + savings + investments) reaches this amount would be required to pay Zakat under the gold standard.

What Is the Silver Nisab?

The Weight

The silver Nisab is 612.36 grams of silver (also written as 52.5 tola or 21 oz). Again, this weight is fixed.

The Value in INR (2026)

Silver prices in India are currently around ₹85–₹95 per gram.

  • At ₹85/g → Silver Nisab = 612.36 × ₹85 = approx. ₹52,051
  • At ₹95/g → Silver Nisab = 612.36 × ₹95 = approx. ₹58,174

So the silver Nisab in India right now is roughly ₹52,000 to ₹58,000. This is a much lower threshold. Anyone with combined savings, cash, gold, and other zakatable assets worth more than this amount would need to pay Zakat under the silver standard.

Why Do Scholars Differ on This?

The short answer is that Islamic scholarship developed over centuries, and scholars in different parts of the world applied their reasoning based on the economic conditions of their time.

In the early days of Islam, gold and silver were both used as currency, and their values were closer together. Over time, silver became much cheaper relative to gold. This created a gap — and so the two Nisab values began to differ significantly in money terms.

The Hanafi View (Most Common in India)

Most Muslims in India follow the Hanafi school of thought. Hanafi scholars say that if your wealth is a mix of cash, gold, silver, and other assets, you should use the silver Nisab as your threshold. This is because silver was historically the standard used for everyday transactions, and using it ensures that Zakat reaches more people in need.

The Shafi and Maliki View

Some Shafi and Maliki scholars hold that gold and silver should each be measured separately using their own Nisab. Under this view, if you only own gold, you apply the gold Nisab. If you only own silver or cash, you apply the silver Nisab. Mixed assets are handled differently under these schools.

⚖️ Simple takeaway: If you follow the Hanafi madhab (which most Indian Muslims do), use the silver Nisab as your threshold for all types of wealth — gold, cash, savings, and investments combined.

A Real-Life Example for Indian Muslims

Let's say Ahmed from Hyderabad has the following wealth at the time of calculating his Zakat:

  • Cash in bank: ₹80,000
  • Gold jewellery (100 g): ₹7,50,000
  • Savings in FD: ₹1,20,000
  • Total zakatable wealth: ₹9,50,000

Using Silver Nisab (≈ ₹55,000)

Ahmed's wealth of ₹9,50,000 is well above ₹55,000. Zakat is due. He must pay 2.5% × ₹9,50,000 = ₹23,750.

Using Gold Nisab (≈ ₹6,75,000)

Ahmed's wealth is also above ₹6,75,000. Zakat is still due under this standard too.

Now let's take Sana from Lucknow, who has:

  • Cash in hand and bank: ₹60,000
  • No gold or silver
  • Total zakatable wealth: ₹60,000

Using Silver Nisab (≈ ₹55,000)

Sana's ₹60,000 exceeds the silver Nisab. Zakat is due. She pays 2.5% × ₹60,000 = ₹1,500.

Using Gold Nisab (≈ ₹6,75,000)

Sana's ₹60,000 is far below the gold Nisab. No Zakat would be due under this standard.

This example shows exactly why the choice of Nisab matters — and why the silver Nisab is considered more inclusive and beneficial for the poor.

Which Nisab Is More Beneficial for the Poor?

The answer is clear: the silver Nisab is more beneficial for the poor.

Here is why. Zakat is meant to redistribute wealth from those who have enough to those who are in need. The more people who are required to give Zakat, the more money flows to the poor, the widowed, the indebted, and those struggling in society.

If the gold Nisab is used, only people with wealth above ₹6.5–7 lakh would give Zakat. That excludes a large part of the middle class and lower-middle class — people who may earn a comfortable salary but whose total savings are still below this mark.

With the silver Nisab at around ₹55,000, many more people are included. Even someone with ₹60,000 in savings is contributing ₹1,500 to help the needy. When millions of Muslims across India do this, the collective impact is enormous.

What Most Indian Scholars Say

The majority of Islamic scholars in India — especially those following the Hanafi school — strongly recommend using the silver Nisab. Several major Darul Ulooms and Islamic finance bodies in India have consistently issued guidance in favour of the silver standard for calculating Zakat on mixed wealth.

The general principle is: when there is a choice between an interpretation that benefits the poor more and one that benefits them less, the one that benefits the poor more should be preferred.

So Which Nisab Should You Follow in India?

Here is a simple guide based on your situation:

  • You have only gold jewellery and nothing else → Apply the gold Nisab (87.48 g). If you have less than 87.48 g of gold and no other assets, Zakat is not due.
  • You have a mix of cash, savings, gold, and/or silver → Use the silver Nisab (≈ ₹55,000). If your combined total is above this, Zakat is due at 2.5%.
  • You follow the Hanafi madhab → Use the silver Nisab for all mixed assets. This is the standard recommendation.
  • You are unsure → Consult a qualified Islamic scholar who can guide you based on your specific situation and school of thought.
📌 Remember: The Nisab amount in ₹ changes every year because gold and silver prices change. Always use the current market rate to calculate your Nisab before working out how much Zakat you owe.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the gold Nisab for Zakat in India in 2026?

The gold Nisab is 87.48 grams of gold. Based on current gold prices in India (approximately ₹7,500 per gram), this works out to roughly ₹6,50,000–₹7,50,000. If your total zakatable wealth equals or exceeds this amount, Zakat is due.

What is the silver Nisab for Zakat in India in 2026?

The silver Nisab is 612.36 grams of silver. Based on current silver prices in India (approximately ₹85–₹95 per gram), this works out to roughly ₹52,000–₹58,000. This is the threshold most scholars recommend using for Zakat.

Which Nisab should I use – gold or silver?

Most Islamic scholars, especially those who follow the Hanafi school, recommend using the silver Nisab. The silver Nisab is much lower in value, which means more Muslims are included in the obligation of giving Zakat. This results in more wealth being distributed to the poor.

Can I use gold Nisab if I only own gold jewellery?

If your entire wealth is in gold (and nothing else), you apply the gold Nisab directly — meaning you need at least 87.48 grams of gold for Zakat to be due. But if you have a mix of gold, cash, and other assets, most scholars say you should use the silver Nisab as your threshold.

Does the Nisab change every year in India?

The weight of Nisab never changes — it is always 87.48 g of gold or 612.36 g of silver. But the INR value changes every year because gold and silver prices fluctuate. Always check the current gold and silver rates when calculating your Zakat Nisab.
